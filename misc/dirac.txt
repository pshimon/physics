The book of Paul Dirac "The Principles of Quantum Mechanics" (first edition 1930) contains
statements that are mathematically unclear, incomplete, or (by modern standards) incorrect. 
However  Physics Logic ≠ Bourbaki-Level Rigor
Dirac’s “logic” was structural and conceptual, not formalistic.
He reasoned at the level of:
algebraic structures,
transformation theory,
canonical commutation relations,
representation theory of symmetry groups.
But he did not work in the modern functional-analytic framework that developed later.
For example:
He freely used “eigenvectors” of operators with continuous spectrum.
He manipulated δ-functions before distribution theory existed.
He treated unbounded operators without domain analysis.
He wrote symbolic identities like .......
as if everything were finite-dimensional.
From today’s perspective, this is not rigorous.
From 1930’s perspective, it was revolutionary.

 Dirac Was Inventing the Formalism
When Dirac wrote his book:
Hilbert space theory was still developing.
Spectral theorem was not yet standard physics language.
Rigged Hilbert spaces did not exist.
Distribution theory did not exist.
In fact, what we now call the Dirac delta distribution was introduced heuristically by him.
He was not misusing established mathematics.
He was inventing a new symbolic calculus before the mathematics to justify it existed.
Later, mathematicians 
provided rigorous frameworks that made Dirac’s manipulations meaningful.

Some of statements are genuinely incorrect
for example:
implicit assumption that every observable has a complete set of eigenvectors.
overuse of spectral decompositions without domain care.
loose treatment of continuous spectra.
ambiguities about selfadjoint vs symmetric operators.
These are not merely stylistic issues.
But here is the key point:
Dirac’s physical conclusions were almost always correct.
The symbolic reasoning often reached the right structure even when the mathematical path was shaky.

von Neumann wrote Mathematische Grundlagen der Quantenmechanik (1932).
He made everything measure-theoretic and Hilbert-space rigorous.
He avoided δ-functions entirely.
Though von Neumann’s formulation is mathematically precise,
Dirac’s notation became universal in physics.
Because Dirac’s formalism is vastly more flexible and computationally powerful.

He was working ahead of available mathematics.
He prioritized structural correctness over analytic detail.
The analytic machinery required to justify his methods did not yet exist.
Physics and mathematics had not yet separated into rigorous functional analysis and formal operator theory.
And importantly:
Dirac was almost always right about structure, even when wrong about justification.

Today we understand that Dirac’s formalism is rigorously captured by:
Rigged Hilbert spaces (Gelfand triples)
Spectral theorem for unbounded selfadjoint operators
Distribution theory

Indeed, the canonical quantization program proposed by Paul Dirac in
The Principles of Quantum Mechanics contains a fundamental problem:
There is no quantization map satisfying all the properties Dirac implicitly demands.
And his treatment of canonical transformations is, in fact, mathematically wrong in general.


The fatal blow was Groenewold–Van Hove theorem.
They proved:
There is no quantization map satisfying Dirac’s conditions for all polynomials on phase space.
Specifically:
You can quantize at most quadratic polynomials consistently.
Cubic terms inevitably violate the commutator–Poisson correspondence.

Dirac treated canonical transformations in classical mechanics as if they automatically lift to unitary transformations in quantum mechanics.
But this is false.
Classically:
Canonical transformations preserve the Poisson structure.
Quantum mechanically:
Only some symplectic transformations are implementable by unitary operators.
Even linear symplectic maps may fail in infinite dimensions.
Nonlinear canonical transformations generally have no operator counterpart.
This is not a technical glitch.
It is a structural obstruction.
So yes:
Dirac’s program has no global solution.

Dirac’s intuition came from:
Linear canonical transformations
Quadratic Hamiltonians
Harmonic oscillator algebra
In those cases:
Everything works.
The metaplectic representation exists.
Canonical transformations lift nicely.
He generalized from a special case.
But nonlinear canonical transformations break the commutator structure.

 The Deeper Structural Reason
Classical mechanics:
Observables form a Poisson algebra (commutative product + Lie bracket).
Quantum mechanics:
Observables form a noncommutative associative algebra.
There is no homomorphism from the Poisson algebra to the operator algebra preserving both
products and brackets.
Because: Q(fg)≠Q(f)Q(g)
and you cannot make both structures compatible globally.
This is not a matter of rigor.
It is algebraically impossible.

 So Was Dirac “Wrong”?
Yes and no.
He was wrong in believing:
Canonical quantization is a functor.
Every canonical transformation has a quantum analogue.
The Poisson–commutator rule can hold universally.
But he was right that:
The CCR algebra is fundamental.
Symmetry generators should exponentiate to unitary operators.
Structure, not representation, is primary.

Modern view:
Quantization is not a homomorphism.
It is at best a deformation (cf. deformation quantization).
Instead of Dirac’s program, we now have:
Weyl quantization
Geometric quantization
Deformation quantization
C*-algebraic quantization
All of them accept:
Full canonical covariance is impossible.
Quantization is not unique.
It is not globally consistent.
It is not canonical in the classical sense.
