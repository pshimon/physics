
INTRODUCTION. SUBJECT AND METHOD OF THEORETICAL PHYSICS

Theoretical physics emerged as an independent science in the early decades of the twentieth century. 
Its dual purpose is, first, to discover — on the basis of specific experimental results — the general laws governing a given domain of physical phenomena, 
and second, to indicate how, starting from those general laws, one can predict in advance the expected behavior of physical systems and the outcomes of particular experiments.

At the present stage of our understanding of nature, both of these tasks are so complex and demand such long chains of logical reasoning 
that progress would be almost impossible without technical tools that help to formalize and, to some extent, automate the process of deduction. 
Mathematics provides these tools. For this reason, in both its inductive and especially its deductive parts, theoretical physics relies heavily on mathematical methods. 
This unusually high degree of mathematization — unusual for any natural scientist, including the experimental physicist — is perhaps the most striking feature of theoretical physics. 
For many, it appears to be an insurmountable barrier, yet it is also the most direct path toward understanding how nature is structured.

However, the ways in which mathematics is used, and the character of reasoning in theoretical physics, have their own specific traits. 
These not only prevent us from treating it as a mere branch of mathematics, but sometimes make its methods appear almost opposite to those of mathematics. 
As one Soviet theorist liked to put it: “The mathematician proves, the physicist persuades.”

The difference lies not in the mental makeup of mathematicians and theoretical physicists but in their subject matter. 
A mathematician, in a certain sense, constructs the objects of study at will, limited only by the demands of logical rigor and consistency. 
A physicist, on the other hand, studies nature as it exists independently of us, presented to us in a single instance. 
Thus, the physicist must be more concerned with ensuring that introduced concepts correspond to this unique object of study than with logical elegance or convenience of reasoning. 
In other words, while theoretical physics is an exact science in its methods, it remains, in subject and aim, a natural science par excellence.

This duality determines another trait of theoretical physics: its relation to mathematics as merely an auxiliary tool — a millstone that grinds only what is poured into it, 
and from which one should not demand more accuracy than is contained in the assumptions fed into it.

But those assumptions themselves face a fundamental limitation: nature, the sole object of theoretical physics, is so complex that a complete treatment of virtually any phenomenon 
with all its details is practically impossible with contemporary mathematics. 
Thus, theoretical physics deals not directly with natural objects and their relations, but with artificially constructed models, 
abstracted from irrelevant details and retaining only essential, defining features. 
This is how abstract notions such as perfectly rigid bodies, incompressible fluids, and material points enter physics. 
Choosing the right model — an act not of strict logic but of intuition — becomes crucial, underscoring the importance of intuitive thinking in theoretical physics.

Indeed, constant reliance on intuition is an integral feature of theoretical physics. 
Not only the discovery of general laws but also deriving consequences from them usually proves impossible without creating vivid mental images of processes. 
Purely formal reasoning risks gross errors. 
Particularly dangerous is the temptation to demand precise, exhaustive definitions for every concept and object in a theory: such definitions often rest on seemingly innocent assumptions 
whose physical meaning is unclear. As a result, one may arrive at impeccably logical conclusions that nonetheless bear no relation to the physical problem at hand.

Special mention must be made of approximation. 
All measured physical quantities are determined with some degree of error. 
And even the mathematical problems required for physical predictions, except in the simplest cases, are so complex that only approximate solutions are possible. 
Often we cannot even verify the validity of the approximations we use, since this would require exact solutions we cannot obtain. 
More importantly, it sometimes turns out that we cannot indefinitely increase the precision of measurements or calculations. 
At some point, the very quantities or regularities under study disappear — much like a newspaper photograph that vanishes under too strong a magnifying glass. 
In mathematics, the analogous case would be an asymptotic series whose terms decrease at first but eventually grow again. 
This means that the very existence of certain quantities or functional relationships in physics is itself approximate in character.



CLASSICAL MECHANICS

1. The Place of Mechanics in Physics

As noted in the introduction, the existence of physical “laws” itself is meaningful only within certain approximations. 
Classical mechanics provides a characteristic example.

It arose from generalizations about the motion of material bodies that we encounter in daily experience. 
Through centuries of effort, scientists concluded that in formulating the laws of mechanics one must abstract away from phenomena that play a major role in everyday life — 
specifically, friction — or at least give up describing its mechanism and exact laws.

By the end of the nineteenth century, with the development of kinetic theory, an explanation emerged: 
the laws of mechanics are, strictly speaking, valid only for the microscopic particles — molecules and atoms — of which bodies are composed. 
The applicability of mechanics to macroscopic bodies is only approximate and indirect, arising from statistical averaging. 
The dynamical quantities of macroscopic bodies — coordinates, momenta, energies — are not true dynamical variables, 
but averages of the microscopic ones. 
The remarkable accuracy of macroscopic equations of motion stems from the enormous number of molecules in any macroscopic body, 
which makes fluctuations — deviations from averages — negligibly small.

Thus, for macroscopic mechanics, its laws emerge in a manner that Erwin Schrödinger aptly called “order from disorder.” 
But when the accuracy of observation is increased, these laws break down — Brownian motion provides a well-known example. 
As for frictional forces, we must either leave them undescribed or introduce them artificially as extraneous to mechanics.

The paradox deepened in the twentieth century, when it became clear that for the very microscopic particles making up bodies, 
classical mechanics generally does not apply either: their motion is governed by the laws of quantum mechanics. 
Moreover, at very high speeds the effects of relativity must be taken into account. 
Strictly speaking, then, classical mechanics has no true domain of applicability. 
It exists only thanks to the approximate nature of our descriptions.

Why, then, study classical mechanics? 
Because despite lacking an exact domain, it describes vast ranges of phenomena with excellent approximation. 
Equally important, classical mechanics developed extremely powerful mathematical methods — the tools of analytical mechanics — 
which are so refined that nearly all modern physical theories are built “in their image and likeness.” 
Thus, any introduction to theoretical physics must begin with the methods of analytical mechanics.

In presenting the foundations of analytical mechanics, however, we will emphasize not mathematical rigor for its own sake, 
but rather the connection with our physical understanding of nature.



2. Fundamental Concepts of Mechanics

According to a well-known philosophical principle, space and time are forms of the existence of matter. 
Thus, they belong among the fundamental concepts of theoretical physics. 
Like all physical concepts, their introduction must rest on experimental data and on procedures that associate specific numbers, obtained from measurements, with them.

However, no experiment can directly measure the position of a body in space or the precise instant of an event. 
We can only measure distances between bodies and time intervals between events. 
Therefore, to assign numerical values to space and time, we must choose some body as the origin of spatial distances, 
and some moment as the origin of time intervals.

To measure distances, we use scales — comparing the distance in question with the size of a body chosen as a standard. 
To measure time, we use clocks — any periodic process in which the same state repeats regularly.

What criteria should guide the choice of length and time standards? 
The only decisive one is reproducibility. 
If we compare a large group of rulers or clocks with one another repeatedly, a subgroup will stand out whose internal comparisons show the smallest discrepancies. 
It is rational to adopt one of these as the standard. 
If later comparisons reveal an even better subgroup, we should naturally switch to that. 
Thus, in the actual history of science, the standards of length and time were refined step by step. 
For a long time, the best length standards were specially prepared rigid bodies of macroscopic size, 
while the best clocks were astronomical periodic processes — the rotation of planets and their revolutions around the Sun. 
Recently, with advances in microphysics, better standards have been found in atomic processes, 
and the international standard of length is now based on the wavelength of a particular spectral line in vacuum.

Two curious points arise here. 
First, the length standard is not a thing but a process — electron oscillations in an atom. 
Strictly speaking, this defines a time standard, and the length standard is derived indirectly by multiplying by the fundamental constant of nature, the speed of light in vacuum. 
Second, paradoxically, although we use this process to define length, with current technology we cannot yet use it directly as a time standard: 
we can count how many light waves fit into a meter, but we cannot count how many oscillations occur in a second. 
Conversely, converting wavelength back into frequency is limited by the fact that although the speed of light is an exact fundamental constant, 
its numerical value is not yet known with perfect precision.

Experience shows that to determine the position of one body relative to another requires three independent measurements. 
Hence, space is three-dimensional. 
Further comparisons of experimental facts lead to the conclusion that space is Euclidean, possessing homogeneity and isotropy.

As mentioned earlier, we can measure only relative positions of bodies, never absolute ones. 
The body relative to which we define the positions of others is called the reference body. 
The coordinate system attached to it, together with a clock, is called a frame of reference.

Classical mechanics assumes that space and time are absolute: distances and time intervals are independent of the motion of the chosen frame of reference.



2.3. Simplified Models and the Concept of the Material Point

As noted before, simplified models are essential in theoretical physics. 
One of the most important in mechanics, without which it would be impossible to formulate its fundamental laws, is the concept of the material point. 
A material point is a body so small that its size and internal structure can be neglected, and its only significant property is its position in space.

In macroscopic mechanics, this is an idealization. 
In the microscopic world, however, elementary particles must be treated, within limits, as genuine material points. 
Thus, the concept of the material point is more fundamental than that of an extended body, both for expository convenience and for the actual structure of nature.

In classical (non-quantum) terms, a material point has at each instant a definite position in space, given by its three coordinates or radius vector as a function of time. 
This is itself a physical assumption (not valid in quantum mechanics). 
We further assume that the dependence of the radius vector on time is smooth enough to admit derivatives of sufficient order — again, an assumption not valid in quantum theory.

We then define the velocity of a material point as the time derivative of its radius vector, 
and acceleration as the derivative of velocity, i.e., the second derivative of the radius vector with respect to time.

In what follows, we will mainly discuss the mechanics of systems of material points. 
Constraints will rarely be considered, and if they appear in examples, they will be assumed to be ideal, finite, and holonomic.

For describing motion, it is not necessary to use Cartesian coordinates of all the material points. 
We may instead choose any set of n functions (where n is the number of degrees of freedom) that completely describe the motion. 
These are called generalized coordinates, and their time derivatives are generalized velocities. 
Thus, for a single point, three generalized coordinates are needed; for a system of N unconstrained points, n = 3N.



The Concept of State and Equations of Motion

A central idea in mechanics is the state of a system. 
In classical theory, to specify the state of a system at a given instant means to assign values to enough dynamical variables that all future (and past) values of these variables are uniquely determined. 
Experiment shows that specifying all generalized coordinates and generalized velocities at one instant suffices. 
This is an additional assumption — one could imagine theories with higher-order derivatives, 
and indeed such theories are studied in certain areas of physics. 
But if we accept the assumption, it follows that accelerations must be uniquely determined by the coordinates and velocities at a given instant. 
In other words, the accelerations must be expressible as functions of the coordinates and velocities at that same moment.

Relations of this kind, specific to a system, are called its equations of motion. 
The main task of mechanics is therefore twofold:  
(1) To establish the equations of motion of mechanical systems, and  
(2) To integrate these equations, thereby obtaining the motion explicitly — that is, the dependence of the generalized coordinates on time.



3. The Principle of Least Action

In tackling the first part of mechanics’ main problem, 
we will not follow the historical path of successive generalizations based on accumulating experimental facts. 
(Though let us note that Galileo and Newton laid the physical foundations of mechanics, while Lagrange and Hamilton developed its mathematical form.) 
Instead, we will begin directly with an extraordinarily general principle: the principle of least action.
